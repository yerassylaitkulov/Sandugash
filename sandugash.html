Grammar rules

All grammar topics from your lessons with explanations and examples

Comparison

Comparative & superlative adjectives

Use comparative to compare two things; superlative for the most/least in a group. Also use as…as for equality.

AdjectiveComparativeSuperlative
talltaller thanthe tallest
beautifulmore beautiful thanthe most beautiful
goodbetter thanthe best
badworse thanthe worst
She is taller than her sister. / He is the most competitive player.
As…as: She is as slim as her mother.
Modals

Have to / must / should

Must = strong personal obligation. Have to = external obligation. Should = advice. Negatives change meaning completely.

You must take your medicine. (strong obligation)
I have to be at school by 8. (rule/external)
You should take notes in class. (advice)
You don't have to come. (not necessary — it's optional)
I mustn't forget. (prohibition)
Have got

Haven't got

Have got = possession (British English). Negative: haven't got. Same meaning as "don't have".

I haven't got a sword. = I don't have a sword.
She has got sensitive skin. = She has sensitive skin.
Past tenses

Past Simple vs Past Continuous

Past Continuous: was/were + -ing → action in progress at a specific moment.
Past Simple interrupts it.

I was reading when she arrived.
While he was sleeping, she left.
They were playing football all afternoon.
Past tenses

Past Perfect

Use Past Perfect for the action that happened first, before another past action. Form: had + past participle.

She had already left when I arrived.
By the time the film started, we had found our seats.
He was tired because he had walked all day.
Passive

Passive voice

Focus on the action, not who does it. Form: is/are/was/were + past participle.

Active: People consider this valuable.
Passive: This is considered valuable.
Passive past: The castle was built in 1200.
Refugees are being helped by volunteers.
Present Perfect

Present Perfect — for, since, ever, never, always

Form: have/has + past participle. Use for past actions with a connection to now.

I have lived here for 5 years. (duration up to now)
She has worked here since 2019. (from a point in time)
Have you ever visited London? / I have never eaten sushi.
I have always wanted to travel.
Present Perfect

Present Perfect or Past Simple?

Past Simple: finished time (yesterday, in 2020, last week). Present Perfect: unfinished time or no specific time.

I went to Paris last year. ✓ (finished, specific time)
I have been to Paris. ✓ (experience, no specific time)
She has lost her keys. ✓ (still relevant now)
She lost her keys yesterday. ✓ (finished)
Future

Going to / will / Present Continuous for future

FormUseExample
going toplans & predictions with evidenceI'm going to study tonight.
willspontaneous decisions, promises, offersI'll help you carry that!
Present Continuousfixed future arrangementsI'm meeting her at 6.
Articles

Articles — a / an / the / Ø

a/an = first mention, one of many: I saw a refugee at the border.
the = specific, both speakers know: The refugee I mentioned is safe.
Ø (no article) = plural/uncountable general: Refugees need help. Water is essential.
Intensifiers

So / such / so much / so many

So + adjective/adverb. Such + (a/an) + noun. So much + uncountable. So many + countable plural.

It was so unusual. / She is such a brave person.
There was so much traffic. / There were so many people.
