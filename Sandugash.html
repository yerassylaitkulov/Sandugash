Comparison

Comparative & Superlative

Comparative compares two things. Superlative picks one out of many.

Short adj: fast → faster → the fastest
Long adj: beautiful → more beautiful → the most beautiful
Irregular: good → better → the best; bad → worse → the worst
Comparison

as … as (equality)

Use as + adj/adv + as to say two things are equal. Use not as … as for inequality.

She is as tall as her brother.
This test wasn't as difficult as the last one.
Modals

have to / must / should

must / have to = obligation (necessary). should = advice (a good idea).

You must wear a seatbelt. (rule)
I have to study tonight. (external obligation)
You should drink more water. (advice)
Modals

don't have to vs haven't got

don't have to = no obligation (you can if you want). haven't got / don't have = do not possess.

You don't have to come — it's optional.
I haven't got a pen. = I don't have a pen.
Past tense

Past Perfect

Use had + V3 for something that happened before another past action.

When I arrived, the film had already started.
She had finished her homework before dinner.
Past tense

Past Continuous

Use was / were + V-ing for an action in progress in the past.

I was reading when the phone rang.
They were playing football at 5 o'clock.
Voice

Passive voice

The subject receives the action: be + V3. Use when the action matters more than the doer.

The letter was written yesterday.
English is spoken all over the world.
The thief was captured by the police.
Intensifiers

so / such / so much / so many

so + adj/adv. such + (adj) + noun. so much + uncountable. so many + countable plural.

It's so cold today.
She's such a kind person.
I have so much work today.
There were so many people at the party.

Conditional

First Conditional (real future)

If + Present Simple, will + V. Used for real / possible situations in the future.

If it rains, we will stay at home.
If you study, you will pass the exam.
Conditional

Second Conditional (imaginary)

If + Past Simple, would + V. Used for unreal or imaginary situations.

If I were rich, I would travel the world.
If she knew the answer, she would tell us.
Future

going to / will / Present Simple (future)

Three ways to talk about the future — each with a different flavour:

going to — plan or evidence: I'm going to study tonight. Look at those clouds — it's going to rain.
will — decision made now / prediction: I'll help you. She'll probably win.
Present Simple — timetable / schedule: The train leaves at 6pm.
Present Perfect

for & since

Both used with Present Perfect for duration. for + a period of time. since + a specific starting point.

I've lived here for five years. (period)
I've lived here since 2020. (start point)
She's known him for ages / since childhood.
Present Perfect

always, ever, never

Frequency adverbs go between have / has and the main verb. ever = at any time (questions). never = not at any time.

I've always wanted to visit Paris.
Have you ever been to Japan?
She has never tried sushi.
Present Perfect

Present Perfect vs Past Simple

Present Perfect = experience / unfinished time (ever, never, just, already, yet, for, since). Past Simple = finished time (yesterday, last week, in 2020, ago).

I have visited London. (at some point in my life)
I visited London last year. (finished time)
She has lived here since 2020. / She lived there from 2015 to 2020.
Word formation

Word Formation & Suffixes

English builds new words by adding suffixes to a root. The suffix signals the word's part of speech (noun, adjective, etc.).

Suffixes that make nouns — jobs / people:

SuffixExamples
-er / -orphotograph → photographer, build → builder, translate → translator, invent → inventor, farm → farmer, interpret → interpreter
-istart → artist, economy → economist, piano → pianist
-ianmusic → musician, politics → politician, electric → electrician
-antassist → assistant, account → accountant, reception → receptionist (-ist overlap)

Suffixes that make abstract nouns — -tion / -sion:

invite → invitation
explain → explanation
compete → competition; ambition
discuss → discussion
decide → decision

Adjective suffixes — -ful, -y, -al, -ous, -ive, -ent:

fame → famous
danger → dangerous
health → healthy
differ → different
kind → kindness (noun: -ness)
Pronunciation

Word stress patterns

In many two-syllable words, the noun is stressed on the first syllable and the verb on the second.

REcord (noun) vs reCORD (verb)
PREsent (noun) vs preSENT (verb)

Practice stress in: danger, kindness, critic, artist, difference / healthy, friendly, famous, different / invite, explain, discuss, employ, decide, compete / invitation, explanation, competition, ambition, decision.
