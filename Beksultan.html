Comparison

Comparative & superlative adjectives

Use comparative to compare two things; superlative for the most/least in a group. Use as…as for equality.

AdjectiveComparativeSuperlative
talltaller thanthe tallest
beautifulmore beautiful thanthe most beautiful
goodbetter thanthe best
badworse thanthe worst
She is taller than her sister. / She is as tall as her friend.
Modals

Have to / must / should / don't have to

Must = strong obligation. Have to = external rule. Should = advice. Don't have to = optional (not forbidden).

You must study hard. / You should take notes. / You don't have to come — it's optional.
Have got

Have got / haven't got

Have got = possession (British English). Same meaning as "have / don't have".

I haven't got a pen. = I don't have a pen.
She has got a great idea. = She has a great idea.
Past tenses

Past Simple vs Past Continuous

Past Continuous: was/were + -ing → longer background action.
Past Simple → shorter action that interrupts it.

I was reading when she arrived.
While he was sleeping, the phone rang.
Past tenses

Past Perfect

Use Past Perfect for the action that happened first before another past action. Form: had + past participle.

She had already left when I arrived.
He was tired because he had walked all day.
Passive

Passive voice

Focus on the action, not who does it. Form: is/are/was/were + past participle.

Active: People consider this valuable.
Passive: This is considered valuable.
Passive past: The castle was built in 1200.
Present Perfect

Present Perfect — for, since, ever, never, always

Form: have/has + past participle. Use for past actions with a connection to now.

I have lived here for 5 years.
She has worked here since 2019.
Have you ever visited London? / I have never eaten sushi.
Present Perfect

Present Perfect or Past Simple?

Past Simple: finished time (yesterday, last year, ago). Present Perfect: no specific time, or result matters now.

Past Simple ✓Present Perfect ✓
I went to Paris last year.I have been to Paris.
She lost her keys yesterday.She has lost her keys! (still missing)

Articles

Articles — a / an / the / Ø

ArticleWhen to useExample
a / anFirst mention; one of manyI saw a dog.
theBoth speakers know which oneThe dog barked.
ØGeneral plural / uncountableI love dogs. Water is essential.
Intensifiers

So / such / so much / so many

So + adjective/adverb. Such (a/an) + noun. So much + uncountable. So many + countable plural.

It was so cold. / She is such a kind person.
There was so much snow. / There were so many people.

Prefixes

Well- and Badly- compound adjectives

Use a hyphen before a noun. No hyphen after a verb.

A well-known author. (before noun → hyphen)
The author is well known. (after verb → no hyphen)
A badly-organised event. / The event was badly organised.
