Comparison

Comparative & superlative adjectives

Use comparative to compare two things; superlative for the most/least in a group. Use as…as for equality.

AdjectiveComparativeSuperlative
talltaller thanthe tallest
beautifulmore beautiful thanthe most beautiful
goodbetter thanthe best
badworse thanthe worst
slimslimmer thanthe slimmest
She is taller than her sister. / He is the most competitive player.
As…as: She is as slim as her mother. / This is as valuable as gold.
Modals

Have to / must / should / don't have to

Must = strong personal obligation or prohibition. Have to = external rule. Should = advice. Don't have to = not necessary (it's optional — completely different from mustn't!).

You must take your medicine. (strong — you have no choice)
I have to be at school by 8. (external rule)
You should take notes in class. (advice)
You don't have to come. (optional — it's fine either way)
You mustn't cheat. (prohibition — it's forbidden)
Have got

Have got / haven't got

Have got = possession (common in British English). Negative: haven't got. It means the same as "don't have".

I haven't got a sword. = I don't have a sword.
She has got sensitive skin. = She has sensitive skin.
Have you got a hoodie? = Do you have a hoodie?
Past tenses

Past Simple vs Past Continuous

Past Continuous: was/were + -ing → longer background action.
Past Simple → shorter action that interrupts it. Key words: while, when, as.

I was reading when she arrived.
While he was sleeping, she left.
They were playing football all afternoon.
What were you doing at 8pm yesterday?
Past tenses

Past Perfect

Use Past Perfect for the action that happened first, before another past action. Form: had + past participle. Key words: already, just, by the time, before, after, when.

She had already left when I arrived.
By the time the film started, we had found our seats.
He was tired because he had walked all day.
I had never seen a refugee camp before that visit.
Passive

Passive voice

Use passive when the action matters more than who does it. Form: is/are/was/were + past participle. The doer can be added with by.

Active: People consider this valuable.
Passive present: This is considered valuable.
Passive past: The castle was built in 1200.
Passive continuous: Refugees are being helped by volunteers.
To form: Subject + is/are/was/were + past participle (+ by ...)
Present Perfect

Present Perfect — for, since, ever, never, always

Form: have/has + past participle. Used for past actions connected to the present. Key words: for, since, ever, never, always, just, already, yet.

I have lived here for 5 years. (duration up to now)
She has worked here since 2019. (from a point in time)
Have you ever visited London?
I have never eaten sushi. / I have always wanted to travel.
She has just arrived. / Have you finished yet?
Present Perfect

Present Perfect or Past Simple?

Past Simple: use with finished time (yesterday, in 2020, last week, ago). Present Perfect: no specific time, or when the result matters now.

Past Simple ✓Present Perfect ✓
I went to Paris last year.I have been to Paris. (experience)
She lost her keys yesterday.She has lost her keys. (still missing!)
He lived here in 2010.He has lived here for 5 years. (still here)
Future

Going to / will / Present Continuous for future

FormUseExample
going toplans & predictions with evidenceI'm going to study tonight.
willspontaneous decisions, promises, offersI'll help you carry that!
Present Continuousfixed future arrangementsI'm meeting her at 6.
Look at those clouds — it's going to rain! (evidence now)
A: I'm cold. B: I'll close the window. (spontaneous)
I'm flying to London on Monday. (booked, fixed)
Articles

Articles — a / an / the / Ø

ArticleWhen to useExample
a / anFirst mention; one of manyI saw a refugee.
theBoth speakers know which oneThe refugee I mentioned is safe.
Ø (no article)Plural / uncountable in generalRefugees need help. Water is essential.
She is a brave soldier. (first mention, any soldier)
The soldier saved her. (we know which one)
I love Ø history. / The history of Kazakhstan is fascinating.
Intensifiers

So / such / so much / so many

So + adjective/adverb. Such (a/an) + noun. So much + uncountable noun. So many + countable plural.

It was so unusual. / She is such a brave person.
There was so much traffic. / There were so many refugees.
He speaks so quickly. / It was such a competitive exam.

Prefixes

Well- and Badly- compound adjectives

These attach to past participles to form compound adjectives. Use a hyphen before a noun, no hyphen after a verb.

A well-known scientist. (before noun → hyphen)
The scientist is well known. (after verb → no hyphen)
A badly-managed project. / The project was badly managed.
Other examples: well-paid, well-dressed, badly-organised, badly-behaved
